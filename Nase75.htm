9.1  The definitions of a ring and field

Prototypical example for this section: ℤ, ℝ, and ℂ are rings; the latter two are fields.

I’ll very informally define a ring/field here, in case you skipped the earlier chapter.

  • A ring is a structure with a commutative addition and multiplication, as well as subtraction, like ℤ. It also has an additive identity 0 and multiplicative identity 1.
  • If the multiplication is invertible like in ℝ or ℂ, (meaning makes sense for any x≠0), then the ring is called a field.

In fact, if you replace “field” by “ℝ” everywhere in what follows, you probably won’t lose much. It’s customary to use the letter R for rings, and k or K for fields.

Finally, in case you skipped the chapter on groups, I should also mention:

  • An additive abelian group is a structure with a commutative addition, as well as subtraction, plus an additive identity 0. It doesn’t have to have multiplication. A good example is ℝ3 (with addition componentwise).
